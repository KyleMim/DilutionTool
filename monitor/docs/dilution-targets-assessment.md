# Dilution Target Assessment — 12 Company Deep Dive

**Date:** February 17, 2026
**Methodology:** Each company was scored by our dilution monitoring tool, then verified against SEC filings, earnings reports, and market data via web research.

---

## How to Read This Document

Companies are grouped into three tiers based on our assessment:

1. **High Conviction** — The dilution risk is real, imminent, and confirmed by public data
2. **Real Dilution, But Not Urgent** — Heavy historical dilution, but the company recently raised capital and isn't under immediate pressure
3. **False Positives / Misread** — Our tool flagged them, but the underlying situation doesn't match the score

Within each tier, companies are ordered by conviction level.

### NNE — Nano Nuclear Energy
**Sector:** Nuclear Energy (Micro-Reactors) | **Market Cap:** ~$1.5B | **Score:** N/A (7 dilutive filings flagged)

**The story:** NNE is developing portable micro-nuclear reactors (ZEUS and ODIN) and a nuclear fuel fabrication subsidiary (HALEU Energy). They went public in May 2024 at $4/share and the stock has since risen to ~$30, making them a nuclear energy momentum play.

**Why it was flagged:**
- 7 dilutive SEC filings in under 2 years (the highest filing count in our database)
- IPO (May 2024), two follow-on offerings (Jul + Oct 2024), two private placements (Nov 2024 $60M, May 2025 $105M), a massive $400M private placement (Oct 2025), and a $900M shelf / $400M ATM registration (Jan 2026)
- Share count grew from ~24M to ~50M (doubled)

**Why it's a false positive:**
- The company has **$577.5M in cash** as of December 31, 2025
- Quarterly operating cash burn is just **$4M** (Q1 FY2026)
- Annual burn is ~$16M
- At current burn rates, NNE has **30+ years of runway**
- The $400M ATM program is registered but **completely undrawn** — they have no need to use it
- The stock is up ~7x from IPO, meaning all dilution to date has been at progressively higher prices — shareholders who held through the raises are up enormously

**Assessment:** NNE dilutes from a position of **strength**, not weakness. They raise capital opportunistically when the stock is high and investor demand is strong, not because they're running out of money. This is the opposite of the distressed-dilution pattern our tool is designed to catch. A company with 30 years of cash runway should not be in the same category as one with 1.3 months. Our scoring system penalizes filing frequency without distinguishing between "raising because we'll die without it" and "raising because we can."

**Data quality:** Filing date off by 1 day (Jan 27 vs Jan 28). The real issue is conceptual: the scoring model doesn't differentiate opportunistic vs. distressed dilution.

**Sector:** Cannabis | **Market Cap:** ~$1B | **Score:** 31.9

**The story:** Canopy Growth is a Canadian cannabis company that has been through years of cash burn, management turnover, and restructuring. They recently completed a major debt recapitalization that fundamentally changed their balance sheet.

**Why it was on our radar (recent activity):**

- Shares went from **87M to 378M in ~18 months** — a 4.4x increase

**Why the Feb 6 filing is misread:**
- The February 6 S-3 is **not a new ATM**. It's a warrant-resale registration for 18.7M shares tied to a $150M senior secured loan closed January 8, 2026. The lenders received warrants at $1.30/share, and this filing lets them resell those shares. Canopy receives no proceeds from any resale.

**Assessment:** The dilution is absolutely real — 4.4x share increase — but it's partially driven by debt-to-equity conversion from the recapitalization, not just equity offerings. The current score of 31.9 is relatively low because the cash position improved dramatically post-recap ($371M cash, 51.7-month runway in DB). CGC is interesting as a case study in how our tool handles complex multi-instrument capital structures (ATMs + warrants + debt conversion + resale shelves), but it's not a high-conviction dilution target right now because the balance sheet was substantially repaired in January 2026.

**Data quality:** Feb 6 S-3 misclassified as ATM shelf (is warrant-resale). Share CAGR of 9.1% (3Y) dramatically understates recent dilution velocity.

## Key Takeaways

**1. The best targets share three traits:** depleting cash + active dilution mechanism + no clear path to profitability. BTDR, CAN, and TBN all check these boxes. SOC did too until its recent raises.

**2. High scores don't always mean high urgency.** SLDB (79.0), LXEO (69.3), and QSI (70.0) earned their scores through extreme historical dilution, but all three recently raised large rounds and have 18-31 months of runway. The next dilution event is quarters away, not days.

**3. Filing frequency alone is misleading.** NNE has the most filings in our database but is the least distressed — they have 30+ years of cash. The tool needs to distinguish between "raising because we must" and "raising because we can."

**4. The most actionable signal is the combination of short runway + active ATM + declining revenue.** CAN (Nasdaq delisting + $262M unfired ATM + collapsing Q1 guidance) and TBN (going concern + pre-revenue + serial offerings) are the purest expressions of this.

**5. Post-raise companies move from Tier 1 to Tier 2 quickly.** SOC and EOSE were genuine high-urgency targets 3 months ago. After raising $500M+ and $1B+ respectively, they've bought time. The tool should reflect this more dynamically — currently, scores lag capital events by weeks or months.
